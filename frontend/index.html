What is black hole ray tracing?

Ordinary ray tracing assumes light travels in straight lines. Near a black hole it doesn't — spacetime itself is curved, so light follows geodesics: the straightest possible paths through that curvature. To render an image, we fire one ray per pixel backward from a virtual camera, integrate its bent trajectory through the curved metric, and ask what it hits: the accretion disk, a background star, or the black hole's shadow. The result is a genuine gravitational-lensing image, not a stylized approximation.

The physics behind the simulation

Schwarzschild metric

For a non-rotating black hole of mass M, spacetime curvature is fully described by one metric. Photon paths in the equatorial plane reduce to a single orbit equation in u = 1/r:

d²u/dφ² + u = 3Mu²

The 3Mu² term is the general-relativistic correction — set it to zero and you recover straight-line Newtonian optics.

Key radii

Everything else — the shadow size, the photon ring, the innermost stable orbit — falls out of two numbers:

Gravitational Redshift & Time Dilation

A clock sitting near a black hole ticks slower than one far away — and photons climbing out of a gravity well lose energy and shift toward the red end of the spectrum. Both effects follow directly from the Schwarzschild metric factor f(r) = 1 − 2M/r.

What these numbers mean

Redshift z tells you by how much a photon emitted at radius r is stretched when received at infinity. A photon emitted with wavelength λ arrives with λ · (1 + z). At r → 2M the redshift diverges — the photon's energy goes to zero before it escapes.

Time dilation τ/t is the ratio of proper time (experienced by a stationary observer at r) to coordinate time at infinity. At r = 6M (ISCO) a clock ticks at ≈ 94% of the rate of a distant clock. At the photon sphere (r = 3M) it ticks at ≈ 82%.
